Designing a website that shows the user the way

Lilleskolerne is an association for free elementary schools in Denmark. They have around 60 free schools as members. Each year, they organize a creative festival that revolves around music, dance, theatre, art and socializing in the form of workshops and performances: Lilleskolefestivalen.



CHALLENGE

Lilleskolefestivalen is a recurring event with a different theme each year. As the members of Lilleskolerne take turn arranging the festival, it is planned and executed differently. There seems to be no consistency in the representation of the festival and there’s content that might be difficult to find.



SOLUTION

To achieve consistency and to maintain that for each year, a website will be developed with relevant content no matter the theme. The focus is on information architecture and visual hierarchy.

MY ROLES

Content Production, UI Design, Front-end Development



LOCATION

Copenhagen, Denmark

View Prototype

THE BACKBONE OF THE WEBSITE

Before we started sketching, we identified and defined the content and the relationships between it by affinity diagramming. We then conducted open card sorting with test users to see how much this differed from our perspective. Some of them gave us new insight which inspired how we labelled the website pages. From this point on, the information architecture pattern could be realized.


Due to this project’s amount of content, we went a lot more in depth of information architecture than in other projects. I learned that the design of an information architecture pattern is extremely important and essentially the bridge between research and prototyping.

THE FRONT-END EXECUTION

Since this was a fairly big website everyone in our team (we were 3) was involved in the front-end solution; each person was assigned one or two pages. Of course, everyone has their own way of coding so a few things ended up looking a bit different from each other.


Midway coding we realized how we should have discussed what exact numbers to use for padding and margin and which responsive values. A style guide is what we needed.

CONNECTING TO A CMS

This project had to be realised with a headless CMS (Storyblok). Data had to be retrieved through the native REST API and displayed on the website. We were introduced to JavaScript not long ago and so we did not manage to connect to Storyblok.


Nonetheless, this experience sparked my interest to try a different CMS, such as Umbraco, as I think the use of a CMS is extremely useful.
